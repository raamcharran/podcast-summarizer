Episode Thesis

Peter Thiel argues that the Enlightenment's post-Westphalian settlement — which suppressed integrative political-theological thinking as too dangerous — has itself become the greater danger in a century of nuclear weapons, AI, and biotech. Drawing heavily on René Girard's scapegoat theory and Carl Schmitt's friend-enemy distinction, he contends that 'muddling through' without confronting deep questions of political theology leads either to creeping one-world totalitarianism (the Antichrist archetype) or civilizational collapse, and that AI's inherently centralizing nature makes this reckoning urgent. His proposed escape route synthesizes Girardian unveiling, Lutheran decentralization, and libertarian anti-sacrificial individualism into a political theology that resists both Calvinist fatalism and utopian global governance.

Chapter [ 01 ]

Defining Political Theology in Late Modernity

THIELCOWEN
★★★☆☆
Interviewer

Cowen opens by tracing the term 'political theology' from Bakunin's mockery of Mazzini through Carl Schmitt's adoption of it as a universal necessity, then directly asks Thiel what the term means to him — probing whether Thiel sees it as descriptive or prescriptive.

Treaty of WestphaliaSchmittian Political TheologyEnlightenment SpecializationSocratic Turn to Common SenseMultiversity vs. Universe

Political theology is framed as a corrective to late modernity's hyper-specialization — Thiel's 'Adam Smith's pin factory on steroids' — where individuals become smaller cogs unable to think about the whole. The Enlightenment abandoned integrative political-theological thinking not because it was futile but because it was deemed too dangerous and divisive, a settlement Thiel traces to the 1648 Treaty of Westphalia. Thiel argues the 21st century has inverted that calculus: it is now more dangerous not to think holistically, and draws a structural parallel between the failing 'multiversity' and the 'crazed multiverse' to ground his Socratic, human-scale starting point.

I think that might have been a reasonable compromise in the 18th century. It's my view that when you fast-forward to the 21st century, it's maybe more dangerous not to think about things.
Chapter [ 02 ]

Peter Thiel's Religious Identity and Anti-Calvinism

THIELCOWEN
★★★☆☆
Interviewer

Cowen challenges Thiel's self-description as 'religious but not spiritual' by arguing this is inherently Calvinist — concrete, serious, skeptical of claims about heaven — and presses why Thiel doesn't simply adopt Calvinism or, alternatively, slide into Catholicism/Eastern Orthodoxy/Mormonism where free will is affirmed, questioning the stability of his middle position.

Girardian ScapegoatingFive-Point CalvinismThomistic Intellect-Will DistinctionEA RationalismProtestant Decentralization

Thiel rejects Calvinism on two grounds: Girardian anthropology frames it as extreme scapegoating where 'Mr. God' bears all blame, and its doctrine of total depravity undermines the possibility of rational discourse. He draws a parallel between Calvinism and EA/rationalist communities, arguing both claim rationality outwardly while internally embracing epistemic helplessness ('spaghetti code'). He defends his Lutheran Protestant position as aligned with decentralized, libertarian political theology, dismissing Catholicism with 'my two-word rebuttal is Pope Francis' and arguing the Reformation necessarily came from outside the institution.

Isn't Calvinism just an extreme form of scapegoating, where Mr. God did everything, he determined everything? He's why you're wearing that blue jacket, and everything you did wrong — it's all Mr. God's fault.
Chapter [ 03 ]

The Old Testament's Influence on Political Thought

THIELCOWEN
★★★☆☆
Interviewer

Cowen directly challenges whether the Old Testament's victim-siding logic leads inevitably to wokeness — asking why Thiel shouldn't simply endorse the woke position as the Bible's logical conclusion. He also presses on what Thiel meant at Harvard by telling the left to 'relativize its victimhood' and probes whether any holy books beyond the Bible inform his thinking.

Girardian Scapegoat TheoryPolitical TheologyHigher CriticismVictimhood AbsolutismNietzschean Ressentiment

Thiel argues the Bible consistently inverts perspective to side with victims — Cain/Abel mirrors but reverses Romulus/Remus, and the Exodus is told from the Israelites' rather than Egypt's viewpoint, following René Girard's framework. He maps three competing political-theological positions: a Christian middle path (history is terrible but demands forgiveness), a woke position (history is terrible, drop forgiveness), and a Nietzschean/Bronze Age Pervert position (reject historical guilt entirely). On victimhood, Thiel proposes Christ as the upper bound — claiming greater victimhood than Christ signals lost perspective, and 19th-century 'higher criticism' that over-separated Old from New Testament invariably became anti-Semitic.

There is a woke version, where the history's terrible, but we're going to forget about the forgiveness part. Then there is maybe a right-wing Nietzschean, Bronze Age Pervert alternative, which is, we're going to forget about the history.

Chapter [ 05 ]

Millenarian Thought and Existential Risks Today

THIELCOWEN
★★★☆☆
Interviewer

Cowen asks what Schmitt fundamentally missed, then challenges Thiel's implicit linearity by raising the cyclical perspective — liberalism collapsed in Weimar but rebounded in West Germany by 1948, suggesting Schmitt's prediction of permanent liberal exhaustion was historically wrong.

Schmitt's Friend-Enemy DistinctionReagan CoalitionScapegoat MechanismWeimar LiberalismPolitical Theology

Thiel draws parallels between Weimar Germany's exhausted liberalism and the 2020s US, arguing that deep questions of political theology suppressed since the Enlightenment are resurfacing. He illustrates Schmitt's friend-enemy distinction through the Reagan coalition — the millionaire, the general, and the priest had nothing in common except anti-communism, and the coalition disintegrated once the Berlin Wall fell and the common enemy vanished. Thiel critiques Schmitt's blind spot: politics may function as a scapegoating machine that only works when participants lack self-awareness, so rationally describing the friend-enemy division may actually accelerate political disintegration rather than strengthen it.

If you say, 'We're having a lot of conflicts in our village, and we have to find some random elderly woman and accuse her of witchcraft, so that will achieve some psychosocial unity as a village,' this sort of thing doesn't really work if you're that self-aware.
Chapter [ 06 ]

Why Muddling Through Leads to Disaster

THIELCOWEN
★★★☆☆
Interviewer

Cowen asks whether the stakes are now too high for cyclical history because civilizational collapse may be unrecoverable, then challenges Thiel by proposing that the current era mirrors the English 17th century's millenarian thought — scientific revolution, political upheaval, diverse theologies — and asks whether this is a new millenarian age.

Linear vs. Cyclical HistoryBook of Daniel's Four KingdomsThucydidean TrapMillenarianismExistential Risk from Technology

Thiel argues for a Judeo-Christian linear view of history over the Greco-Roman cyclical model, contrasting Thucydides' repeatable war narratives with the Book of Daniel's unique succession of four kingdoms. He insists that nuclear weapons, AI, bioweapons, and environmental degradation make the present fundamentally unlike any prior era, rendering cyclical analogies trivially false. When Cowen proposes a parallel between today and the English 17th-century millenarian ferment, Thiel rejects it as another illegitimate cyclical frame, countering that modern apocalyptic fears stem from secular science and technology rather than religious fervor.

I would just start by saying they're very different. We're in a very different world than we were in 1900. I don't know how you unlearn all the knowledge we've gained, even since 1900.
Chapter [ 07 ]

The Katechon as Restraining Force Against Tyranny

THIELCOWEN
★★★☆☆
Interviewer

Cowen's framing pushed Thiel to articulate his 'anti-anti-tech' position — how to navigate between the Scylla of technological existential risks and the Charybdis of political totalitarianism without defaulting to either techno-optimism or millenarian panic.

KatechonGirardian ApocalypticismAntichrist as Political ArchetypeOne-World TotalitarianismMillenarian Fearmongering

Thiel argues that a one-world totalitarian government belongs on the list of existential risks alongside nuclear weapons, AI, climate change, and biotech — and may be more urgent because, in biblical eschatology, the Antichrist precedes Armageddon. He cites René Girard's observation that the Catholic Church stopped preaching end-times sermons after 1945 to decouple nuclear weapons from apocalyptic mythology, even as the bombs carried mythic names like Trinity, Saturn, and Jupiter. Drawing on two pre-WWI Antichrist novels — Robert Hugh Benson's Lord of the World (1908) and Solovyov's War, Progress, and the End of History (1900) — Thiel contends that a post-1945 version of the Antichrist would seize power precisely by weaponizing millenarian fear of Armageddon to justify global political consolidation.

In my mind, one other existential risk is a one-world totalitarian government. I find that at least as scary as the others.

AI's Impact on Math People Versus Word People

THIELCOWEN
★★★★☆
Interviewer

Cowen asks whether 'wordcels' — writers, pundits, idea-people — will simply lose all influence as LLMs grow more powerful, and whether they'll give up power peacefully or 'set off nuclear bombs.' He frames it as a direct challenge to the conventional tech assumption that language-oriented thinkers are most vulnerable to AI displacement.

French Revolution meritocracyWordcel vs shape-rotatorMath Olympiad automationSoviet chess grandmaster elevationGatekeeping via quantitative testing

Thiel argues AI will hurt math people far more than word people, inverting the conventional Silicon Valley assumption — within 3-5 years, AI models will solve all US Math Olympiad problems, making math ability a commoditized rather than gatekeeping skill. He traces the societal bias toward math testing to the French Revolution's egalitarian impulse (verbal ability ran in families, math was distributed randomly) and suggests the Soviet elevation of number theorists and chess grandmasters was actually a control mechanism, since math people are 'singularly clueless' about the world. Silicon Valley's extreme bias toward math ability as a universal filter — using physics and calculus to gate medical school admission — is the thing most likely to be reversed by AI.

My intuition would be it's going to be quite the opposite, where it seems much worse for the math people than the word people.

Straussian Messages and Satan in the Bible

COWENTHIEL
★★★☆☆
Interviewer

Cowen opens with a deliberately enormous prompt — 'What are the Straussian messages of the Bible and what do they tell us about political theology?' — framing the entire chapter as a challenge to synthesize esoteric hermeneutics with political theory in one answer.

Straussian esotericismGirardian scapegoat mechanismApocalypse as unveilingLibertarian political theologyTemptation of Christ

Strauss and Girard both recognized that unveiling hidden truths about the social order can destroy it — the Greek 'apocalypse' literally means unveiling, making Revelation inherently deconstructive. To see Satan is to see Satan fall, so the only time Satan appears in the Bible is at the very end of the world.

Sovereign Individual Thesis and Technology's Direction

THIELCOWENAUDIENCE MEMBER
★★★☆☆
Interviewer

Cowen challenged Thiel on whether he would bet on open-source AI as a test case for decentralization's supposed advantages — more dynamism, more innovation, greater safety. This directly probed whether Thiel's stated preference for decentralization holds when applied to the concrete AI landscape.

The Sovereign Individual thesiscentralization-decentralization cyclesp(doom) / EA rationalismLincoln's Lyceum Addressopen-source AI

Thiel traces a cyclical pattern in computing: centralized in the 1960s (HAL 9000, Star Trek supercomputers running entire planets), radically decentralized by the late 1990s per the Reese-Mogg/Davidson 'Sovereign Individual' thesis, then re-centralizing by the late 2010s — with crypto as the lone exception. He argues these trajectories are not predetermined and rejects Calvinist-style p(doom) fatalism, asserting he fears the humans powerful enough to stop AI more than AI itself, since 'a force that's powerful enough to stop the AI is probably a force that's powerful enough to destroy the world, too.' The Q&A also touches Ayaan Hirsi Ali's utilitarian conversion to Christianity (Thiel prefers focusing on truth over utility) and Lincoln's Lyceum Address, where Thiel reads Lincoln as self-identifying with the 'towering genius' Caesar-Napoleon figure.

I'm much more worried about the humans trying to stop the AI than the AI destroying us. A force that's powerful enough to stop the AI is probably a force that's powerful enough to destroy the world, too.
